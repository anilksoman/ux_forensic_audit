00 The Method

Why "topology"?

From mathematics: the study of spatial relationships and how surfaces connect.

Applied to UX, topology means we map the shape of the problem, not just the symptoms. Most audits produce a list of design opinions. The Fault Topology Method produces a forensic intelligence report — with quantified business impact, root causes, and a clear prioritised remediation sequence.

The word "forensic" is deliberate too. Forensics means applying structured method to recover truth from evidence. We do not ask "what does this look like?" We ask "what does the evidence prove — and what does it cost?"

"The output is not a list of design opinions. It is a forensic intelligence report with quantified business impact and a clear implementation roadmap."

The Dual-Track Principle

Most UX audits run either heuristic evaluation (expert judgment) or data analysis. The Fault Topology Method runs both simultaneously and then looks for convergence and divergence between them.

Convergence — heuristic finding confirmed by data
→ High confidence faultline
Divergence — heuristic finding contradicted by data
→ Interesting anomaly requiring deeper investigation
Data without heuristic explanation
→ Hidden faultline
Heuristic without data signal
→ Potential future faultline or measurement gap
01 1–3 days

Reconnaissance

Establishing the forensic baseline. Before investigating a crime scene, you document the scene as found.

The Reconnaissance phase is about understanding what the product thinks it is versus what users actually experience — before any evaluation begins. This prevents the most common audit failure: evaluating against the wrong criteria.

1.1 — Stakeholder Alignment Sessions

Purpose: Understand what success looks like from every internal perspective — which are often in conflict with each other.

Product Leadership
  • What does this product do in one sentence — for the user, not the pitch deck?
  • Where do you believe users struggle most?
  • What metrics concern you that you cannot explain?
  • What has been tried before to fix problems — and what happened?
  • What would "fixed" look like in measurable terms?
Engineering Leadership
  • Where are the known technical debts that affect user experience?
  • What are the most frequent user-reported bugs?
  • What does your error logging show about where things break?
  • Where does the product behave differently than designed?
Customer Success / Support
  • What are the top 10 support ticket categories?
  • What do users say when they cancel or churn?
  • What workarounds have users invented?
  • What questions come up in every onboarding call?
Sales Team
  • What objections do prospects raise about the product?
  • What features do prospects ask for that don't exist?
  • What do lost deals cite as the reason?
Data / Analytics
  • Where are the biggest drop-off points in the funnel?
  • What is the retention curve shape — and where does it inflect?
  • Which features have lowest adoption despite being core to the value proposition?
  • Where do users spend unexpected amounts of time?
Deliverable
  • Stakeholder Perception Map — documenting where internal perspectives align, conflict, and have blind spots
  • Internal misalignment itself creates product faultlines — this becomes a forensic exhibit in the final report

1.2 — Artifact Collection

Purpose: Gather every document that shaped the product to understand the gap between intention and reality.

What is collected

  • Original PRDs and design system documentation
  • Existing user research reports
  • Support ticket logs (last 90 days minimum)
  • App store / G2 / Capterra reviews (last 6 months)
  • NPS data and verbatim comments
  • Onboarding completion rates
  • Feature adoption analytics
  • A/B test results (past 12 months)
  • Competitor comparison documents

The forensic lens on artifacts

Reviews and support tickets are read not for what users say but for what users cannot name. Users rarely identify the root cause of their frustration — they describe symptoms.

Example: “This is confusing” → forensic reading traces it to a mental model mismatch in navigation structure, not a button label.

Deliverable: A Pre-Existing Evidence Register — cataloguing all available data, rating quality and recency of each source, and flagging where critical data is absent. No data about a critical flow is itself a finding.

Purpose: Create the quantitative fingerprint of the product's current health before any qualitative evaluation begins.

The Forensic Significance Test — for each metric, ask:

Is this metric moving in the same direction as all others? (Divergence signals a specific faultline)
Does this metric behave differently for different user segments? (Reveals who is being underserved)
Has this metric changed at a specific date? (Date-correlated changes reveal what caused the problem)
What metric is conspicuously absent from reporting? (What nobody measures is often where the biggest problems hide)

Deliverable: A Quantitative Baseline Report — the product's vital signs before investigation. This becomes the comparison document for measuring remediation impact later.

02 5–10 days

Investigation

The Dual-Track Evaluation Engine — where forensic audit diverges most fundamentally from standard UX review.

Track A — Heuristic Evaluation: Three-Tier System

Your framework does not just use Nielsen's 10 (which every auditor uses). It applies three layers of heuristic evaluation that elevate this to strategic forensics.

The classic 10 heuristics applied with forensic specificity — not just "does this violate the heuristic" but "what is the business cost of this violation."

H1
Visibility of System Status
Forensic extension: Where does status invisibility create anxiety, duplicate actions, or support tickets?
  • Can users always locate themselves within the product?
  • Are loading, processing, and error states clearly communicated?
  • Do background processes (sync, save, calculation) have visible indicators?
  • Where does the absence of feedback cause users to repeat actions?
  • Are confirmation states (success, completion, submission) unambiguous?
What is the estimated support cost of status confusion in this product?

Heuristics developed from 23 years across industries — not in any textbook. This is where the FTM differentiates from every standard audit.

B1
Trust Coherence
Does every interaction reinforce trust — or do specific moments break the trust covenant?
  • Where does the product ask for more than it needs from users?
  • Are there moments where the product's behaviour cannot be explained from a user's perspective?
  • Are there dark patterns — default opt-ins, hidden costs, misleading progress indicators?
  • Do visual design choices signal the appropriate level of seriousness for the domain?
  • Where does the product feel like it is serving the business at the user's expense?

⚡ Rooted in a real trust breakdown at PayPal: merchants perceived unfairness in dispute outcomes despite a fair system, silently eroding adoption — this heuristic was built from that diagnosis.

What is the trust debt this product is accumulating — and what will it cost when it is called?

Drawing on cognitive science, behavioural economics, and predictive processing theory — the intellectual layer that separates the FTM from generalist auditors.

P1
Relevance Realization
From: John Vervaeke's cognitive science framework. Does the product help users determine what is relevant to their current goal — or does it present everything and demand users sort it themselves?
  • Does the interface surface what is most relevant to the user's current context?
  • Is relevance determined by user behaviour and history — or is it static for all users?
  • Does the product's information architecture reflect relevance hierarchies or categorical hierarchies?
