00 The Method

Why "topology"?

From mathematics: the study of spatial relationships and how surfaces connect.

Applied to UX, topology means we map the shape of the problem, not just the symptoms. Most audits produce a list of design opinions. The Fault Topology Method produces a forensic intelligence report — with quantified business impact, root causes, and a clear prioritised remediation sequence.

The word "forensic" is deliberate too. Forensics means applying structured method to recover truth from evidence. We do not ask "what does this look like?" We ask "what does the evidence prove — and what does it cost?"

"The output is not a list of design opinions. It is a forensic intelligence report with quantified business impact and a clear implementation roadmap."

The Dual-Track Principle

Most UX audits run either heuristic evaluation (expert judgment) or data analysis. The Fault Topology Method runs both simultaneously and then looks for convergence and divergence between them.

Convergence — heuristic finding confirmed by data
→ High confidence faultline
Divergence — heuristic finding contradicted by data
→ Interesting anomaly requiring deeper investigation
Data without heuristic explanation
→ Hidden faultline
Heuristic without data signal
→ Potential future faultline or measurement gap
01 1–3 days

Reconnaissance

Establishing the forensic baseline. Before investigating a crime scene, you document the scene as found.

The Reconnaissance phase is about understanding what the product thinks it is versus what users actually experience — before any evaluation begins. This prevents the most common audit failure: evaluating against the wrong criteria.

1.1 — Stakeholder Alignment Sessions

Purpose: Understand what success looks like from every internal perspective — which are often in conflict with each other.

Product Leadership
  • What does this product do in one sentence — for the user, not the pitch deck?
  • Where do you believe users struggle most?
  • What metrics concern you that you cannot explain?
  • What has been tried before to fix problems — and what happened?
  • What would "fixed" look like in measurable terms?
Engineering Leadership
  • Where are the known technical debts that affect user experience?
  • What are the most frequent user-reported bugs?
  • What does your error logging show about where things break?
  • Where does the product behave differently than designed?
Customer Success / Support
  • What are the top 10 support ticket categories?
  • What do users say when they cancel or churn?
  • What workarounds have users invented?
  • What questions come up in every onboarding call?
Sales Team
  • What objections do prospects raise about the product?
  • What features do prospects ask for that don't exist?
  • What do lost deals cite as the reason?
Data / Analytics
  • Where are the biggest drop-off points in the funnel?
  • What is the retention curve shape — and where does it inflect?
  • Which features have lowest adoption despite being core to the value proposition?
  • Where do users spend unexpected amounts of time?
Deliverable
  • Stakeholder Perception Map — documenting where internal perspectives align, conflict, and have blind spots
  • Internal misalignment itself creates product faultlines — this becomes a forensic exhibit in the final report

1.2 — Artifact Collection

Purpose: Gather every document that shaped the product to understand the gap between intention and reality.

What is collected

  • Original PRDs and design system documentation
  • Existing user research reports
  • Support ticket logs (last 90 days minimum)
  • App store / G2 / Capterra reviews (last 6 months)
  • NPS data and verbatim comments
  • Onboarding completion rates
  • Feature adoption analytics
  • A/B test results (past 12 months)
  • Competitor comparison documents

The forensic lens on artifacts

Reviews and support tickets are read not for what users say but for what users cannot name. Users rarely identify the root cause of their frustration — they describe symptoms.

Example: “This is confusing” → forensic reading traces it to a mental model mismatch in navigation structure, not a button label.

Deliverable: A Pre-Existing Evidence Register — cataloguing all available data, rating quality and recency of each source, and flagging where critical data is absent. No data about a critical flow is itself a finding.

Purpose: Create the quantitative fingerprint of the product's current health before any qualitative evaluation begins.

The Forensic Significance Test — for each metric, ask:

Is this metric moving in the same direction as all others? (Divergence signals a specific faultline)
Does this metric behave differently for different user segments? (Reveals who is being underserved)
Has this metric changed at a specific date? (Date-correlated changes reveal what caused the problem)
What metric is conspicuously absent from reporting? (What nobody measures is often where the biggest problems hide)

Deliverable: A Quantitative Baseline Report — the product's vital signs before investigation. This becomes the comparison document for measuring remediation impact later.

02 5–10 days

Investigation

The Dual-Track Evaluation Engine — where forensic audit diverges most fundamentally from standard UX review.

Track A — Heuristic Evaluation: Three-Tier System

Your framework does not just use Nielsen's 10 (which every auditor uses). It applies three layers of heuristic evaluation that elevate this to strategic forensics.

The classic 10 heuristics applied with forensic specificity — not just "does this violate the heuristic" but "what is the business cost of this violation."

H1
Visibility of System Status
Forensic extension: Where does status invisibility create anxiety, duplicate actions, or support tickets?
  • Can users always locate themselves within the product?
  • Are loading, processing, and error states clearly communicated?
  • Do background processes (sync, save, calculation) have visible indicators?
  • Where does the absence of feedback cause users to repeat actions?
  • Are confirmation states (success, completion, submission) unambiguous?
What is the estimated support cost of status confusion in this product?
H2
Match Between System and Real World
Forensic extension: Where do terminology mismatches create support escalations or onboarding failures?
  • Does the product use jargon internal to the company rather than natural to the user?
  • Are metaphors and icons consistent with how users understand the domain?
  • Do labels describe what things ARE or what they DO?
  • Are error messages written in user language or system language?
  • Do confirmation dialogues reflect the user's mental model?
What is the churn contribution of mental model mismatch?
H3
User Control and Freedom
Forensic extension: Where does the absence of control create rage clicks, session abandonment, or trust destruction?
  • Can every action be undone within a reasonable window?
  • Are destructive actions (delete, cancel subscription, clear data) reversible or clearly warned?
  • Can users exit any flow without losing progress?
  • Are users ever trapped in a sequence they cannot exit without completing?
  • Does the back button/gesture always work as expected?
H4
Consistency and Standards
Forensic extension: Where does inconsistency create cognitive load that accumulates into abandonment?
  • Are the same actions triggered by the same patterns throughout the product?
  • Do similar components behave identically across different contexts?
  • Is the product consistent with platform conventions (iOS, Android, Web)?
  • Are there areas of the product that feel like different products (indicating team or era boundaries)?
H5
Error Prevention
Forensic extension: What is the operational cost of preventable errors — support tickets, data loss, failed transactions?
  • Are confirmation steps present before irreversible actions?
  • Are form fields validated in real-time with specific guidance, not just error states?
  • Are dangerous actions separated from common actions spatially?
  • Are there places where a single misclick causes disproportionate damage?
H6
Recognition Over Recall
Forensic extension: Where does excessive recall demand create power-user dependency and new-user failure?
  • Are all available actions visible or easily discoverable without memorisation?
  • Do users need to remember information from one screen to use another?
  • Does the product penalise infrequent users who have forgotten the interface?
  • Are there hidden features users need to be told about to discover?
H7
Flexibility and Efficiency of Use
Forensic extension: Where does rigid linearity create expert user frustration and novice user abandonment simultaneously?
  • Can experienced users bypass unnecessary steps?
  • Are keyboard shortcuts, bulk actions, and power features available?
  • Do advanced features interfere with novice users' ability to find basic functions?
H8
Aesthetic and Minimalist Design
Forensic extension: Where does visual noise increase cognitive load enough to affect task completion rates?
  • Does every element on each screen earn its presence?
  • Is there information presented that users do not need at the moment of decision?
  • Does progressive disclosure work — revealing complexity only when needed?
  • Does the visual hierarchy direct attention to what matters?
H9
Help Users Recognise, Diagnose, and Recover From Errors
Forensic extension: What percentage of support contacts could be eliminated by better error communication?
  • Do error messages identify what went wrong in user language?
  • Do they explain why it went wrong and tell users exactly what to do next?
  • Are errors distinguished by severity — informational, warning, critical?
  • Do error states preserve user input rather than clearing forms?
H10
Help and Documentation
Forensic extension: What is the failure demand (support contacts caused by interface failure) versus value demand (contacts for legitimate complex needs)?
  • Is contextual help available at points of likely confusion?
  • Are help articles written for users' questions or the company's product structure?
  • What percentage of support tickets reference topics the interface should handle independently?

Heuristics developed from 23 years across industries — not in any textbook. This is where the FTM differentiates from every standard audit.

B1
Trust Coherence
Does every interaction reinforce trust — or do specific moments break the trust covenant?
  • Where does the product ask for more than it needs from users?
  • Are there moments where the product's behaviour cannot be explained from a user's perspective?
  • Are there dark patterns — default opt-ins, hidden costs, misleading progress indicators?
  • Do visual design choices signal the appropriate level of seriousness for the domain?
  • Where does the product feel like it is serving the business at the user's expense?

⚡ Rooted in a real trust breakdown at PayPal: merchants perceived unfairness in dispute outcomes despite a fair system, silently eroding adoption — this heuristic was built from that diagnosis.

What is the trust debt this product is accumulating — and what will it cost when it is called?
→ See B1 applied: PayPal Trust Audit
B2
Cognitive Load Architecture
How does the product distribute cognitive demand across the user journey — and where does it ask too much at the worst moment?
  • Where in the journey does the product require the most cognitive effort?
  • Does this align with where users have the most motivation (early) or least (mid-journey fatigue)?
  • Are complex decisions front-loaded into onboarding when users have least context?
  • Does the information architecture reflect the user's cognitive model or the product team's org structure?
Where does cognitive overload convert into abandonment — and what is the revenue cost of each abandonment point?
B3
Feedback Loop Integrity
Does the product create healthy feedback loops that build user competence and confidence — or anxiety loops that erode both?
  • Does using the product make users feel more capable over time?
  • Are there features that users avoid because past experience taught them to fear them?
  • Does the product celebrate user progress and milestone achievement?
  • Does the product communicate the consequences of actions before they are taken?
Which features have the highest avoidance rate — and what does that avoidance cost in retention and expansion revenue?
B4
Cross-Channel Coherence
Does the experience remain coherent as users move between touchpoints — web, mobile, email, support, sales?
  • Does the product remember context across sessions and devices?
  • Do email communications use the same language and mental models as the interface?
  • Does the support experience align with interface promises?
  • Does the mobile experience complement rather than truncate the web experience?
Where do cross-channel inconsistencies create the highest support load and churn risk?
B5
Failure Mode Dignity
When things go wrong — and they always do — does the product treat users with dignity?
  • When the product fails (downtime, error, data loss), how does it communicate?
  • Are users left in states of uncertainty during failures?
  • Does the product apologise for its failures or blame users?
  • Are failure states designed with the same care as success states?
What is the NPS impact of failure states — and how does the dignity of failure handling affect recovery?
B6
Scale-Induced Debt
Where has the product accumulated design decisions that made sense at a smaller scale but create faultlines at current scale?
  • Are there interface patterns that work for 100 items but break at 10,000?
  • Are there admin interfaces designed for small teams now used by hundreds?
  • Where has the product been extended beyond its original design envelope?
  • What has been bolted on versus genuinely integrated?

⚡ Applied at Cisco to surface design debt across 6 product teams — patterns that worked for a prototype had become systemic drag at enterprise scale.

What is the operational cost of scale-induced UX debt — in support time, training costs, and efficiency loss?
→ See B6 applied: Cisco Design Debt Audit
B7
Adoption Gravity
Does the product create conditions for expanding user adoption — or does it inadvertently resist it?
  • How does the product encourage users to discover more of its value over time?
  • Does the product's value proposition become clearer or more opaque as users engage more deeply?
  • Are sharing, collaboration, and expansion features prominent or buried?
Where is adoption gravity weakest — and what is the expansion revenue being left uncaptured?

Drawing on cognitive science, behavioural economics, and predictive processing theory — the intellectual layer that separates the FTM from generalist auditors.

P1
Relevance Realization
From: John Vervaeke's cognitive science framework. Does the product help users determine what is relevant to their current goal — or does it present everything and demand users sort it themselves?
  • Does the interface surface what is most relevant to the user's current context?
  • Is relevance determined by user behaviour and history — or is it static for all users?
  • Does the product's information architecture reflect relevance hierarchies or categorical hierarchies?
→ See P1 applied: Waterloo Expert Paralysis case
P2
Predictive Processing Alignment
From: Karl Friston's predictive brain framework. Does the product behave in ways that align with users' predictive models — or does it consistently violate predictions, generating surprise and friction?

Core insight: The brain is fundamentally a prediction machine. When interfaces consistently violate predictions, the cognitive cost accumulates into what users describe as "confusing" or "frustrating" — even when they cannot articulate why.

  • Do interface elements behave as their visual appearance predicts?
  • Are there elements that look interactive but are not — or vice versa?
  • Where does the product require users to update their mental model unexpectedly?
  • Are there flows where the next step is unpredictable from the current step?
P3
Loss Aversion Architecture
From: Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory. Does the product use loss aversion appropriately — to motivate beneficial actions — or does it weaponise it to manipulate users?
  • Are loss framings used to communicate genuine risk — or to create artificial urgency?
  • Do progress indicators represent real progress — or manufactured commitment?
  • Are free trial endings and subscription renewals communicated with appropriate advance notice?
  • Does the product use scarcity honestly?
P4
Cognitive Load Distribution
From: John Sweller's cognitive load theory. How is cognitive load distributed across intrinsic (task complexity), extraneous (interface complexity), and germane (learning investment) dimensions?
  • Where does the interface add extraneous cognitive load on top of inherently complex tasks?
  • Are there tasks where interface complexity exceeds task complexity — meaning the interface is harder than the problem?
  • Does the product scaffold complex tasks — breaking them into manageable cognitive chunks?
  • Where does the product fail to offload cognitive work it could handle for the user?
P5
Agency and Autonomy
From: Self-determination theory (Deci and Ryan). Does the product create experiences of genuine user agency — or an illusion of choice while constraining actual autonomy?
  • Do users feel in control of their data, their preferences, and their experience?
  • Are choices meaningful or manufactured?
  • Does the product respect user decisions — or repeatedly override or question them?
  • Where does the product's business interest conflict with the user's expressed preferences?

Track B — Algorithmic & Data Forensics

Where Track A applies expert judgment, Track B applies structured data analysis to find faultlines that expert judgment alone cannot see.

In Practice The Amazon case study shows Track B in fullest form: funnel forensics, support ticket vocabulary analysis, and session behaviour analysis all converging on a single structural faultline. Read The Defect Diagnosis Faultline →
B.1 — Funnel Forensics

Revenue Topology Map

Map all funnels (not just conversion — include activation, feature adoption, support resolution, and expansion). Assign revenue value to each drop-off: Drop-off × ACV = Revenue Impact. Segment by user type, device, acquisition channel. Time-stamp to reveal what caused the problem.

B.2 — Behaviour Pattern Analysis

Behaviour Gap Map

Rage click analysis (false affordances), dead zone analysis (features that failed to communicate value), navigation pattern mining (designed path vs actual path), session recording patterns (hesitation, reversal, tab-opening), return visit behaviour (what users believe the product's core value is).

B.3 — Support Ticket Forensics

Support Intelligence Report

Calculate Failure Demand % — typically 30–60% of all support contacts are caused by product failure (and almost always invisible to product teams). Build Interface-Support Correlation Matrix. Extract vocabulary of confusion. Map the Workaround Index.

B.4 — Competitive Benchmark

Competitive Forensic Map

Run identical tasks on 3–5 competitors. A faultline that exists in your product but not competitors is a competitive vulnerability. A faultline that exists across all competitors is a market opportunity — solving it creates genuine differentiation.

B.5 — Performance & Technical UX

Performance Faultline Report

Core Web Vitals forensics (LCP, INP, CLS) on highest-traffic, most conversion-critical, and highest support-ticket-generating pages. Slow is a design problem, not just an engineering problem.

Confidence Levels

Confirmed (High) — found by both heuristic and algorithmic analysis

Indicated (Medium) — found by one track, plausible in the other

Suspected (Low) — found by one track, contradicted or absent in the other

Anomaly — data signal present with no heuristic explanation — requires further investigation

Why this matters

Convergence = high confidence faultline — action it now.

Divergence = hidden risk or measurement gap — watch it.

Anomaly = the most interesting finding in any audit. Something the data shows that no heuristic explains. This is where forensic instinct earns its money.

03 3–5 days

Diagnosis

Building the Fault Topology. This is where investigation becomes intelligence.

3.1 — Faultline Classification: Five Types

Every finding from Phase 2 is classified into one of five Faultline Types — connecting UX problems to business consequences. The fault map plots them on two axes: user journey stage (X) and faultline depth (Y).

Type 1
Surface

Visible usability problems creating immediate friction

Examples: unclear labels, missing feedback Effort: Low–Medium Fix time: Days–weeks
Type 2
Structural

IA or navigation problems making the right action hard to find

Examples: org-structured nav, buried critical functions Effort: Medium–High Fix time: Weeks–months
Type 3
Trust

Interactions that erode user trust through dark patterns or unexplained behaviour

Examples: unclear data usage, unexpected charges Risk: Churn, reviews, regulatory Fix time: Weeks–months
Type 4
Systemic

Deep structural problems rooted in foundational design decisions affecting the entire product

Examples: design system debt, cross-platform incoherence Effort: Very High Fix time: Months–quarters
Type 5
Strategic

Misalignment between what the product is designed to do and what users actually need

Examples: features built on unvalidated assumptions Effort: Highest — requires strategy Fix time: Quarters–reorientation

Reading the Topology

A product with problems concentrated in Onboarding at Surface level has a fixable first impression problem.

A product with problems concentrated in Engagement at Systemic level has a fundamental rethink requirement.

A product with Trust faultlines distributed across the entire journey has a coherence and honesty problem at the design philosophy level — which transforms the remediation conversation from tactical to strategic.

3.2 — Root Cause Analysis: The 5-Why Forensic Method

For each Critical and High severity faultline, a structured root cause analysis is conducted — adapted from manufacturing quality control for organisational UX diagnosis.

The Five Whys — UX Forensic Form

Why does the user fail at this step?
Why does the interface create this condition?
Why was the interface designed this way?
Why was that design decision made?
Why was that organisational condition present that allowed this decision? (Almost always reveals org or process root causes — not just design failures.)

Deliverable: A Root Cause Register — for each critical faultline, a documented chain from symptom to root cause, with the organisational implication named explicitly.

04 3–5 days

Remediation

The Prioritised Fix Architecture. A forensic report without a clear remediation roadmap is intelligence without action.

4.1 — RICE-F Prioritisation

Every faultline is scored on four dimensions — with a Forensic Severity multiplier — to produce a prioritised remediation sequence. This ensures highest-value, highest-confidence, lowest-effort fixes are addressed first.

R
Reach
How many users are affected?
1 (edge case) → 5 (all users)
I
Impact
If fixed, improvement per user?
1 (marginal) → 5 (transformative)
C
Confidence
Certainty of diagnosis and fix?
1 (suspected) → 5 (confirmed)
E
Effort
Resource required to fix?
1 (very high) → 5 (very low)
F
Forensic Severity
Surface ×1.0 / Structural ×1.2 / Trust ×1.5 / Systemic ×1.3 / Strategic ×1.4
RICE-F Score = ( R × I × C × E × F )

4.2 — Three-Horizon Remediation Roadmap

Horizon 1
0–30 days

Immediate Relief

Criteria: High confidence + Low effort + Clear impact. These are not guesses — they are identified through forensic rigour.

Typical fixes: Label and copy changes, error message rewrites, missing feedback states, broken interaction patterns, accessibility failures, performance quick wins.

Deliverable format: Specific, actionable tickets ready for developer implementation — no ambiguity about what needs to change. Each card includes exact current state, exact recommended change, heuristic violated, business impact, implementation spec, and success metric.

Horizon 2
30–90 days

Structural Repair

Criteria: Medium-high confidence + Medium effort + Significant impact.

Typical fixes: Information architecture restructuring, navigation pattern redesign, onboarding flow revision, feature consolidation, trust signal improvements, cross-platform coherence.

Deliverable format: Design briefs with the Before/After Hypothesis:

"Currently, [X% of users] are [failing/abandoning/missing] [specific action] because [root cause]. By [specific design change], we predict [metric] will improve by [range] within [measurement window]."
Horizon 3
90+ days

Strategic Reconstruction

Criteria: Systemic and strategic faultlines requiring product strategy involvement. These cannot be fixed by design alone.

Typical fixes: Core mental model realignment, design system overhaul, value proposition clarification, fundamental navigation architecture change, cross-channel experience unification.

Deliverable format: Executive-level strategic briefs with: The Faultline → The Evidence → Cost of Inaction → Options (with different resource requirements) → Recommendation → Success Metrics.
