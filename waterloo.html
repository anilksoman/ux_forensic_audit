Phase 1 Reconnaissance

01The Business Context

A tool that was technically accurate — but cognitively violent.

The Invisible Tax on Expert Time

Waterloo's 25‑person engineering team specified air diffusers and grilles for commercial buildings across North America. Every day, each engineer made approximately 6 product selections from a catalog of 400+ models, matching performance curves to project‑specific airflow, noise, and pressure requirements. The task happened 120 times per month per engineer — and the tool they used was quietly consuming their expertise.

The existing system was a collection of static PDF performance charts — well‑organised as a technical reference, but completely broken as a decision‑making interface. Every selection forced engineers to mentally interpolate values across 3‑5 parameters simultaneously, hold numbers in working memory while flipping between 14 separate documents, and filter the 400+ products manually based on project context.

The real cost nobody had named: At 18 minutes per selection, 120 selections per engineer per month, the team was losing 900 engineering hours per month to cognitive friction — hours that could not be recovered by working faster because the interface itself was the speed limit. New engineers required 16 weeks of mentorship before they could select independently. The gap between novices (22 min, 17% error rate) and experts (12 min, 4% error rate) was not knowledge — it was perceptual efficiency. And the tool provided zero support to develop it.

Four Faultlines Named

  1. Fragmented data architecture: 14 separate PDFs with inconsistent formatting. Each comparison required up to 8 context‑switches between documents.
  2. Mental interpolation demand: Charts showed discrete performance points. Engineers manually estimated values between curves — an average of 4 interpolation judgments per selection with no visual guidance.
  3. No contextual filtering: All 400+ products presented equally regardless of project parameters. Engineers did the filtering manually, consuming an extra 5‑7 minutes per specification.
  4. Institutional knowledge dependency: The interface embedded no expert heuristics. New engineers required 16 weeks of mentorship. Knowledge lived in people's heads, not in the system — fragile, unscalable, invisible to management.
Phase 2 Investigation — Dual Track

02The Investigation

Where standard research watches. Forensic research interrupts at the point of hesitation.

"They were not reading the charts. They were pattern‑matching against them." — The insight that changed the entire design direction

Track A — Heuristic Evaluation

Expert Shadowing with Cognitive Interrupt Protocol

Over 15 live selection sessions, we paused engineers each time they hesitated, flipped between PDFs, or reached for a calculator. The question: "What are you trying to figure out right now?" The responses revealed that senior engineers were not processing numbers linearly — they were using curve shape, slope, and relative position as heuristic shortcuts. The charts were being read as visual filters, not algebraic data sources. The interface supported algorithmic precision (Layer 1) but completely ignored heuristic pattern recognition (Layer 2) and relevance filtering (Layer 3).

Novice‑Expert Comparison

New engineers (under 2 years): 22 minutes per selection, 17% error rate. Senior engineers (10+ years): 12 minutes, 4% error. The gap was perceptual efficiency — experts had learned to see what mattered. The interface provided no mechanism to develop that skill.

Track B — Algorithmic / Data Forensics

Error Archaeology

Reviewing 18 months of specification revisions (1,840 correction requests), we found 78% of errors clustered at interpolation points — precisely where the interface demanded mental calculation rather than providing direct guidance. This correlated perfectly with the heuristic finding: the highest‑frequency cognitive demand was also the primary error source.

Time‑and‑Motion Analysis

We mapped the selection process end‑to‑end for 340 selections. Data navigation (flipping between PDFs) consumed 31% of total selection time. Interpolation judgments consumed 24%. Together, these two cognitively‑induced activities accounted for more than half of the 18‑minute average.

The Systemic Faultline — Why It Was the Most Expensive

The knowledge‑transfer faultline was not just a training problem. It was an organisational intelligence leak. Every new engineer had to rediscover what the previous generation had already learned — 16 weeks of reinvention each time. At an average salary of $85,000, those 16 weeks represented approximately $26,000 in direct training cost per new hire. Multiplied across 4‑6 new engineers annually, that was $100,000‑$150,000 spent on a problem the interface could have solved for a fraction of the cost.

Phase 4 Remediation

04The Remediation

Not adding features. Removing cognitive burden — systematically, at each faultline.

The remediation principle: The design solution was not about adding functionality. It was about eliminating extraneous cognitive load. Every intervention was traced back to a specific faultline, with a forensic rationale grounded in cognitive science.

Forensic RationaleEliminates the highest‑frequency cognitive demand in the workflow and the primary source of selection errors. Curve shape and slope are preserved so expert pattern‑recognition skills remain applicable — serving both algorithmic precision and heuristic efficiency.
