Phase 1 Reconnaissance — and how it began

01The Business Context

A tool that was technically accurate — but cognitively violent. And a 26-year-old designer who noticed something that changed everything.

How This Engagement Happened

In 2004, Ideafarms — a small interactive design studio founded in New Delhi — landed its first significant client: Waterloo Air Management, a commercial HVAC manufacturer producing diffusers and grilles for North American building projects. The brief was not a UX audit. There was no such thing as a UX audit in that sense. The brief was to improve a product selection tool that engineers were using to specify products for project bids.

There was no formal method. No heuristic framework. No dual-track investigation process. What there was: a designer who paid attention to what people actually did with interfaces, rather than what they said they did.

The founding question of what became the FTM: The engineers were using the same data. The same PDF charts. The same 400-product catalog. But a senior engineer could select in 12 minutes with a 4% error rate, and a junior engineer took 22 minutes with a 17% error rate. The interface was identical for both. So why was the experienced engineer faster? The answer to that question — found in an HVAC manufacturer's engineering room in 2004 — became the seed of everything that followed.

The Invisible Tax on Expert Time

Waterloo's 25‑person engineering team specified air diffusers and grilles for commercial buildings across North America. Every day, each engineer made approximately 6 product selections from a catalog of 400+ models, matching performance curves to project‑specific airflow, noise, and pressure requirements. The task happened 120 times per month per engineer — and the tool they used was quietly consuming their expertise.

The existing system was a collection of static PDF performance charts — well‑organised as a technical reference, but completely broken as a decision‑making interface. Every selection forced engineers to mentally interpolate values across 3‑5 parameters simultaneously, hold numbers in working memory while flipping between 14 separate documents, and filter the 400+ products manually based on project context.

The real cost nobody had named: At 18 minutes per selection, 120 selections per engineer per month, the team was losing 900 engineering hours per month to cognitive friction — hours that could not be recovered by working faster because the interface itself was the speed limit. New engineers required 16 weeks of mentorship before they could select independently. The gap between novices (22 min, 17% error rate) and experts (12 min, 4% error rate) was not knowledge — it was perceptual efficiency. And the tool provided zero support to develop it.

Four Problems Named

These were not called "faultlines" in 2004. That vocabulary came two decades later. But the problems identified were the same ones the FTM now classifies as structural and systemic faultlines.

  1. Fragmented data architecture: 14 separate PDFs with inconsistent formatting. Each comparison required up to 8 context‑switches between documents.
  2. Mental interpolation demand: Charts showed discrete performance points. Engineers manually estimated values between curves — an average of 4 interpolation judgments per selection with no visual guidance.
  3. No contextual filtering: All 400+ products presented equally regardless of project parameters. Engineers did the filtering manually, consuming an extra 5‑7 minutes per specification.
  4. Institutional knowledge dependency: The interface embedded no expert heuristics. New engineers required 16 weeks of mentorship. Knowledge lived in people's heads, not in the system — fragile, unscalable, invisible to management.
Phase 2 Investigation — before it had a name

02The Investigation

There was no dual-track protocol in 2004. There was observation and data. That turned out to be enough.

"They were not reading the charts. They were pattern‑matching against them." — The observation that changed the entire design direction. And, looking back, the first moment the forensic instinct fired.

What follows is a reconstruction of the investigative process in FTM terms. The two tracks — heuristic observation and data analysis — were not called that at the time. But the structure was the same: watch what people do, then look at what the numbers confirm.

Track A — Observational (what the FTM would now call Heuristic Evaluation)

Session Observation — Pausing at the Point of Hesitation

Over 15 live selection sessions, engineers were observed as they worked. The key technique: pausing each time someone hesitated, flipped between PDFs, or reached for a calculator. The question asked each time: "What are you trying to figure out right now?"

The responses revealed something that was not in any usability textbook at the time. Senior engineers were not processing numbers linearly — they were using curve shape, slope, and relative position as heuristic shortcuts. They were reading the charts the way a musician reads a score — not note by note, but as a pattern. The charts were being used as visual filters, not algebraic data sources.

Junior engineers, not yet having developed that perceptual fluency, were processing everything linearly. Same interface, completely different cognitive load. The interface supported precise calculation but gave no path to developing pattern recognition. It was actively preventing expertise from transferring.

In FTM terms, this would later be called: The interface served the algorithmic layer (Layer 1 — precise calculation) but completely ignored the heuristic layer (Layer 2 — pattern recognition) and the relevance layer (Layer 3 — contextual filtering). All three layers are required for expert decision-making. Designing for only one is how you create expert paralysis. This understanding was the foundation of what became the FTM's psychological heuristic layer — P1 through P5.

Novice‑Expert Comparison

The gap was measured, not estimated. New engineers (under 2 years): 22 minutes per selection, 17% error rate. Senior engineers (10+ years): 12 minutes, 4% error rate. The gap was perceptual efficiency — experts had learned to see what mattered. The interface provided no mechanism to develop or transfer that skill. It was an institutional knowledge leak dressed up as a data tool.

Track B — Data Analysis (what the FTM would now call Algorithmic / Data Forensics)

Error Archaeology — 18 Months of Revision Requests

Reviewing 18 months of specification revisions (1,840 correction requests), a pattern appeared that confirmed the observation: 78% of errors clustered at interpolation points — precisely where the interface demanded mental calculation rather than providing direct guidance. The highest-frequency cognitive demand was also the primary error source. This was not a coincidence. It was a causal relationship between interface design and failure.

Time‑and‑Motion Analysis

340 selections were mapped end‑to‑end. Data navigation (flipping between PDFs) consumed 31% of total selection time. Interpolation judgments consumed 24%. Together, these two cognitively‑induced activities accounted for more than half of the 18‑minute average. Neither was necessary. Both were created by the interface.

The Systemic Faultline — Why It Was the Most Expensive

The knowledge‑transfer faultline was not just a training problem. It was an organisational intelligence leak. Every new engineer had to rediscover what the previous generation had already learned — 16 weeks of reinvention each time. At an average salary of $85,000, those 16 weeks represented approximately $26,000 in direct training cost per new hire. Multiplied across 4‑6 new engineers annually, that was $100,000‑$150,000 spent on a problem the interface could have solved for a fraction of the cost.

Phase 4 Remediation — the prototype that became a commercial product

04The Remediation

Not adding features. Removing cognitive burden — at each problem point. The solution was delivered as a working prototype that Waterloo converted into a commercial product.

The remediation principle — then and now: In 2004, the framing was simpler: stop making engineers do cognitive work the tool could do for them. The FTM later codified this as "eliminate extraneous cognitive load at each faultline." The principle is the same. Every intervention below traces to a specific problem, with a rationale grounded in how experts actually think.
